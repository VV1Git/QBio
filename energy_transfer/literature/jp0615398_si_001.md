## **Supplementary material** 

The atomic partial charges for a planar chlorophyll _a_ and bacteriochlorophyll _a_ , given in Table I and Table II, respectively, were obtained from a fit of the electrostatic potential of the transition density, and the charge densities of the ground and excited state, obtained in HF-CIS and TDDFT/B3LYP quantum chemical calculations, as described in detail in the text. 

Table III contains the transition charges used in the TrEsp (HF-CIS) calculations in Fig. 10 in the text. The charges were obtained from a fit of the electrostatic potential of the transition density, taking into account the conformations of the pigments. (A geometry optimization constraining some of the torsional angles to their values in the crystal structure was performed.) The charges of hydrogens in the _CH_ 2 and _CH_ 3 groups were constraint to be equal in the fit of the potential. 

Fig. 1 contains a comparison of the excitonic coupling in the ’special pair’ in PS-I obtained in the TDC and TrEsp methods, using TDDFT/B3LYP quantum chemical calculations for the transition densities, and a cube size of (0.2[˚] A)[3] in the TDC calculations. The TrEsp charges used in the calculation of the couplings are given in Table IV. The convergence of the couplings in the two methods is of the same quality as the one obtained with HF-CIS calculations for the same cube size, shown in the text in Fig. 10. 

Fig. 2 shows the basis set dependence of the excitonic coupling for the special pair in PS-I, obtained with TrEsp using HF-CIS quantum chemical calculations for the transition density. It is seen that there is convergence for the 6-31G* basis set. 

In Fig. 3 the ground state charge density coupling between BChl1 and BChl2 of the FMO complex is compared, using Mulliken charges and the charges calculated from a fit of the electrostatic potential of the ground state charge densities of the two pigments. The difference between the two couplings shows that Mulliken charges do not reproduce the electrostatic potential of a molecule very well.
